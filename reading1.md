  <h1>Reading 1: "In Plato’s Cave" – Susan Sontag</h1>

  <p>Despite being published nearly fifty years ago, Susan Sontag’s “In Pluto’s Cave” remains a profound piece relevant today.
     In a world shaped by the constantly consuming internet, there is never enough perspective, as each one helps build the full picture. 
     The “insatiability of the photographing eye” is a trait we’ve adopted with the rise of documenting our daily lives. Photography has 
     changed significantly over the past fifty years; the camera is no longer saved for special moments. Embedded in the device we carry 
everywhere and always within reach, a camera is now used even in casual scenes. We share our successes and conceal our losses, a key aspect 
of social media’s meticulousness. Photos of someone at a nice restaurant, a beach scene, or displaying a new designer bag all intentionally 
convey information. This aligns with Sontag’s claim that “photography makes us feel that the world is more available than it really is.” 
We’re one click away, one photo away, one viral moment away from hitting the jackpot, fueled by the rush of the camera’s flash. The moments I choose to preserve, especially the ones I decide to share, are deliberate. They shape my view of the world, forming a narrative that reflects my environment. The reading caused me to reflect on why certain moments over others deserve to be captured. Intentionally highlighting a fleeting moment encourages me to chase more memories, seek new places, and engage in unfamiliar experiences. As I reintegrate into the graphic world of Illustrator, I’m enthralled with the creative 
freedom I’m able to express.</p>
